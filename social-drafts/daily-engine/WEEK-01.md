## Day 1 (Mon) — X — the price bug

I shipped a bug that cost me money for a week and I never saw it.

My AI receptionist is $297 a month. Stripe charged $297. My website said $297.

But the trade landing pages said $197. So did the FAQ schema. So did the file
ChatGPT reads to learn about my company.

So the AI told people $197. Then they hit checkout and saw $297.

I did not have a pricing problem. I had four sources of truth and no single one.

Fixed it today. Now every price on the site is derived from one table. If I
change the table, all thirty-something surfaces move together. If I ever type
a price by hand again, it is a bug.

Check your own site. Ask ChatGPT what you charge. I bet it is wrong.

## Day 1 (Mon) — LinkedIn — same story, operator framing

A customer asked ChatGPT what my company charges. ChatGPT told them $197 a month.

My actual price is $297.

Here is how that happened, and why I think most small businesses have the same
problem right now without knowing it.

Every website has a file most owners have never heard of. Some AI crawlers read
it to learn what you sell. Mine had last quarter's price sheet in it. Nobody
proofreads a file nobody looks at.

Meanwhile my checkout page was correct, my pricing page was correct, and my
sales scripts were correct. Four surfaces agreed. Three others did not, including
the one the AI was actually reading.

The lesson is not "check your llms.txt." The lesson is that AI assistants are
now quoting your prices to your customers, and they are reading files your
marketing team does not own.

I fixed mine by making every price on the site derive from a single table.
One number, one place, and every page follows.

If you sell anything online, go ask ChatGPT what you charge. Reply and tell me
if it got it right. I am genuinely curious what the hit rate is.

## Day 2 (Tue) — LinkedIn — why the demo IS the pitch

I stopped writing sales copy for my AI receptionist. I made it call people instead.

You type your business name. About sixty seconds later your phone rings. You pick
up and hear a receptionist answering as your company, in a natural voice, trained
on your services. You can try to trip it up. Most people do.

Nothing sells a voice product like a voice.

The economics are the part other founders ask me about: each demo costs me
41 cents. That is the whole customer acquisition experiment. I can afford to
give away a genuinely impressive thing because the thing itself is cheap and
the impression is not.

The old funnel was: ad, landing page, form, call, demo, proposal, close.
The new one is: hear it, decide.

If you are building anything where the product demos better than it pitches,
stop protecting it behind a form. The demo is the marketing.

## Day 6 (Sat) — LinkedIn — the honest one

Revenue check, out loud, because I think founders lie about this by omission.

I have shipped more than a dozen products this year. Real ones, live, that work.

The revenue does not match the shipping. Not close.

Here is what I actually learned, and it took me embarrassingly long: I do not
have an idea problem or a build problem. I have a distribution problem. Every
product I own requires me to go find the customer. None of them pull.

Building is the part I am good at, so building is the part I kept doing. That
is not strategy. That is preference wearing a strategy costume.

So this quarter the rule changed. Nothing new gets built until the things that
exist can be found. This week that meant shipping 28 landing pages that had
been sitting finished and switched off, and fixing every place my prices
disagreed with each other.

Less fun. Almost certainly right.

If you are a builder who keeps building, check the same thing I did. Count your
products. Then count your channels. If the first number is bigger, you already
know what to do.
